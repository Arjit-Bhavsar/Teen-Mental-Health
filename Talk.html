Positive Self-Talk

Learn to recognize negative thought patterns and transform them into thoughts that support your growth and wellbeing.

Why Self-Talk Matters

Positive self-talk has been linked to higher life expectancy, reduced risk of cardiovascular disease, lower pain levels, and better mental health outcomes.

Your inner voice shapes your reality. When you constantly criticize yourself, you train your brain to see threats and failures everywhere. When you speak to yourself with kindness and encouragement, you build resilience and confidence.

The good news: self-talk is a habit, and habits can be changed. With practice, you can rewire how you respond to challenges.

The Core Strategy

Identify negative self-talk, then shift your viewpoint to a more positive outlook.

Example: Turn "I always let my team down" into "I always try my best each game."

This is not about lying to yourself or ignoring problems. It is about being fair to yourself--giving yourself the same grace you would give a friend.

Four Types of Negative Thinking

Recognize these patterns so you can catch them in action.

1

Personalizing

Blaming yourself for everything, even things outside your control.

Problem: "The project failed because of me."
Solution: Identify what YOU specifically did wrong, not what others did.
2

Magnifying

Focusing only on the negative aspects while ignoring the positives.

Problem: "I got one B, my grades are ruined."
Solution: Also acknowledge what went well.
3

Catastrophizing

Expecting the worst to happen without any logical reason.

Problem: "If I fail this test, my life is over."
Solution: Apply logic and consider how you can handle the situation.
4

Polarizing

Seeing things as all good or all bad with no middle ground.

Problem: "I am either perfect or a total failure."
Solution: Find the opportunity and middle ground in every situation.

Reframing in Action

See how negative thoughts can be transformed into helpful ones.

Negative

"I am terrible at math."

Reframed

"Math is challenging for me, and I am working to improve."

Negative

"Nobody likes me."

Reframed

"Making friends takes time. I have people who care about me."

Negative

"I will never be good enough."

Reframed

"I am enough as I am, and I am always growing."

Growth vs. Fixed Mindset

Your mindset--your beliefs about your abilities--shapes everything. A growth mindset means believing you can develop and improve.

Fixed Mindset

  • Believes abilities are set in stone
  • Avoids challenges to prevent failure
  • Gives up easily when things get hard
  • Sees effort as pointless
  • Feels threatened by others success

Growth Mindset

  • Believes abilities can be developed
  • Embraces challenges as opportunities
  • Persists through obstacles
  • Sees effort as the path to mastery
  • Finds inspiration in others success
